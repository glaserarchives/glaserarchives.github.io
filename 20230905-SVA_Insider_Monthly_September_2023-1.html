🎙️ Get To Know: Francis DiTommaso, director of SVA Galleries

Congratulations on your forthcoming retirement from SVA at the end of September. Can you summarize your SVA journey up to this point?

Summarizing a 32-year career in just a few words is a pretty tall order, but here goes. It was a constant drive to provide exhibitions that were more: more numerous, more professional, more educational, more rewarding, and more widely recognized vehicles for students to present their work during their time at SVA. I wanted exhibitions to be a valuable learning experience that would remain with them even after graduation and into their professional lives.


In what ways has SVA changed the most? The least?

Most changed: an extensively expanded infrastructure, a much larger physical plant, vastly improved facilities and amenities, and also the sheer number and variety of majors and disciplines. The least changed is the SVA spirit, the common bond widespread among those working here that inspires enthusiasm, devotion and a strong camaraderie.


Of all the projects you've worked on at SVA—including moving the College's off-campus gallery from SoHo to Chelsea, overseeing the Masters Series and alumni survey shows, and managing a calendar of 40 exhibitions each year—is there any one accomplishment you're particularly proud of?

In broad strokes, your question mainly covers it—plus several exhibitions with a societal bent like “Testimony to War” and “Art as Witness,” as well as retrospectives for Milton Glaser and Marshall Arisman. But on a personal level, as a long-ago art student myself, I derive the most satisfaction from having provided thousands of art students the opportunity to exhibit their work in professional-quality galleries. This was only possible with the steadfast support of the College and with the two dozen high-functioning yet easy-going staff members of SVA Galleries whom I had the privilege to lead over these many years.


Can you share a favorite SVA memory that will stick with you forever?

In 2017, Marshall Arisman asked if I could curate his retrospective. A longtime aficionado, I was flattered. Still, I hesitated, unsure I could do justice to his voluminous and varied body of work. As I began to explain my doubts, Marshall cut me off. He looked me straight in the eye and said in his dead-serious baritone, “Francis, you will curate this show.” Well, I did, and it’s one of my very best exhibitions ever.

M.J. O'Toole
