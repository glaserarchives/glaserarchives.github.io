New York City buzzes with energy in the fall, and DSI is filled with activity and enthusiasm as well. Our second-year students are immersed in their thesis projects, in studying innovation, entrepreneurship and collaborative leadership; new students are already deeply into the fundamentals required to become a professional social designer.

We look forward to all the new ideas and growth that will take place this year, and hope you'll join us in doing so.

Welcome, and welcome back.

Every year, the new cohort begins their DSI experience with Bootcamp, where, new students get to know one another — and their second-year counterparts — so they can better work as a team. Social design, as we say, is social, and collaboration is key to leading change. We assemble each cohort to be as diverse as possible, in cultural background and experience, because the variety of world views and expertise make students’ experience here richer, and prepare them for working across cultures after they graduate. This year's class spans from Chicago to Shanghai, from Maharashtra to Massachusetts. They're experts in sociology, industrial design, digital marketing and ethnographic research.

For the first time this year, we added a bootcamp for returning second year students, to help jumpstart their prototyping practice and sweep away any cobwebs that may have collected over the summer. Students began with a scavenger hunt on the streets of New York, then came back the next day to assemble an exhibit in five hours, based on a social challenge they found. It was an exciting day that kicked off the semester with high energy.

DSI Open House

“Nobody leaves the way they came in, in very good ways,” DSI Chair Cheryl Heller told the auditorium.

At this year’s open house, on Oct. 20, faculty, alumni and current students came together to share what Design for Social Innovation is to prospective MFA candidates. Their enthusiasm for social design is exactly what we look for in potential students, too — people who don’t know exactly how they’ll help the world, but who are already fiercely committed to doing so. 

Faculty members stressed that DSI is a place for mutual learning, where creative people are able to forge understanding together. And alumni shared their stories of growth, the overarching theme being: I thought I knew everything. I learned so much more.

For those who couldn’t make it to our NYC open house, we hope you’ll join us on the web. Our international open house video and brochure will be available in the coming weeks, and it will serve to answer your questions about the program. We are, as always, excited to meet a new cohort in the fall.

The Understatement, Volume Two

Last year, we launched a digital publication with the purpose of being “an antidote to bloviation, obfuscation and jargon.”

We are pleased to bring the second volume of this project to life. As Cheryl Heller so eloquently wrote, "Thank you, dear readers, because readers are a somewhat rare breed, to be admired these days. Thank you for wanting more than soundbites about some of the issues most important to our future, and for your own work in helping us create a better one. We've got work to do."

Cheryl Heller speaks at Kyoorius Designyatra conference

Design for Social Innovation Chair Cheryl Heller spoke at the Kyoorius Designyatra conference in Goa, India, and we are so proud to have her representing this program internationally. 

Designyatra is one of the world’s largest and best annual design conferences and has featured some of the biggest names in the creative and communications industries. We are excited to see Cheryl added to their lineup of industry leaders.

A Preview of Class of 2018 Thesis Projects
The DSI Class of 2018 cohort has jumped into their thesis projects, beginning to assess where and how they'll use their skills to create social change. Last month, they presented these ideas to DSI faculty and students. They're exploring how pleasure can be integrated into the sex-ed conversation, how to help youth and young adults who aren't employed or enrolled in school and how to improve the lives of "leftover women" in China (women who decide not to marry while they focus on their careers, and carry a social stigma because of it). Our students are focused on how they can help solve systemic problems. We are interested to see how their research leads to real-world applications in the coming months.

Guest Lecture Series
One of the best parts of this MFA program is being connected to a network of outstanding professionals whose work has challenged old ideas in their fields. This semester, we've brought disability and LGBTQ+ writer Kenny Fries and coral scientist Ruth Gates to speak with our students and community. While experts in completely vastly different fields, their messages have a common thread: We all have agency. We all have the power to explore systems and experiences in different ways.
