As we attempted to process the recent tragedies that took place in Uvalde, Texas and in Buffalo, New York last month, we experienced even more mass shootings across the country.  On Wednesday, June 1, a gunman opened fire in a medical facility in Tulsa, Oklahoma, killing 4 people before taking his own life. Then, just this past weekend, three people were murdered in a shootout in the popular South Street area of Philadelphia. Six more people were killed in shootings in Tennessee, Virginia, Arizona and South Carolina. 


The Uvalde shooting, where an 18 year-old armed with an AR-15 assault rifle entered Robb Elementary School and murdered 19 children and two teachers and wounded 16 others, is especially horrifying. And the clear racist motive behind the Buffalo grocery store shooting is sickening. The recent loss of so many lives, particularly those of children, has sent shockwaves of sorrow, pain and anguish throughout the country. In response to these shootings, many individuals have looked to the US Congress for answers and action. Community advocates, parents, athletes, educators and President Biden are asking for more than thoughts, prayers and sentiments. People are demanding systemic change and action.


A common experience that comes with empathizing with the families and friends of those directly affected by these recent tragedies is something known as secondary trauma, which results from continuous exposure to such tragedies. The effects of secondary trauma may be unfamiliar to you. Signs of secondary trauma generally include, but not limited to, the following:


  • Feelings of hopelessness and helplessness

  • A sense that one can never do enough

  • Hypervigilance

  • Diminished creativity

  • Chronic exhaustion/physical ailments

  • Guilt

  • Fear

  • Anger and cynicism

  • Numbing/inability to empathize


As we grapple with the magnitude and frequency of these mass shootings, we must take the necessary steps to provide for self-care, such as finding opportunities to rest, exercise, express your creativity and seek help if you feel overwhelmed with thoughts about these tragic events.

On-Campus and Off-Campus Resources: 


Students seeking support are encouraged to reach out to Student Health and Counseling Services for short-term counseling services. Current undergraduate and graduate students may request an appointment with one of SVA's licensed therapists by filling out this form or emailing health@sva.edu. 

 

Faculty and staff are encouraged to take advantage of the mental health benefits that are a part of SVA’s health insurance plans. The following state, national, and international resources are available 24 hours a day/7 days a week to anyone who would like support:

 

For people living in New York City, NYC WELL provides free mental health support, information and referrals in English, Mandarin, and Spanish and has access to translators in about 200 languages. Call 1.888.NYC.WELL (1.888.692.9355), text WELL to 65173 or chat here.  

 

SAMHSA Disaster Distress Helpline is a national hotline that provides free counseling to those emotionally distressed by natural or human-caused disasters.

Crisis Text Line offers free, around-the-clock support via text. Text HOME to 741741.
