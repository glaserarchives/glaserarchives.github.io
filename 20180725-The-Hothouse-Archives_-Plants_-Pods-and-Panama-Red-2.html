“Is it possible to be a revolutionary and like flowers?” artist Camille Henrot asks in one of her recent pieces. Proceeding in a vein of wonder concerning the revolutionary force of plants, Taryn Simon engages floral bouquets in a form of institutional critique. Both artists exemplify the political and cultural implications manifested in plants. They become signifiers for the recurring representation of plants in contemporary art and culture.
Plant symbolism and its metaphorical incitements substantiates their protean role in politics, art, and science. Their meaning changes over time: according to recent developments in knowledge, technology and even religion. In occidental thought in particular, nature is represented both as an embodiment of good and the epitome evil. In Ovid’s version of humanity’s golden age, Earth was an abundant and secure source of nourishment and pleasure. Conversely in Christianity, a serpent tempts Eve into eating the forbidden apple and sharing it with Adam, thus, precipitating their expulsion from the Garden of Eden. Indeed, both of these themes became major preoccupations among Renaissance painters. In the Netherlands, for one example, Dutch still-life paintings, Vanitas, represent moral exortations against excessive materialism by implicating the transience of life.
During the Industrial Revolution, the symbolic role of plants intensified, drawing magnified attention to the prevailing nature/culture divide. Religious implications were discarded, for example, favoring images of plants and animals as wild entities. In Joseph Conrad’s Heart of Darkness, the jungle itself embodies dark and savage spirits. Human nature is thereby characterized as a dominating civil force while fauna and flora are comprehended as wild and heedless.
Under our current condition, flora is undergoing radical changes in response to environmental shifts and laboratory practices. From genetic alterations to indoor farming, from foodstuffs and medicinal uses, plants form the mainstay foundation and buttress of human survival. As self-aware creatures they protect their own and engage in masquerading their identities. Recently, it has been noted that plants are akin to animals and governed by a temporal time structure . As sources of nourishment and wonder they possess healing powers and psychoactive properties.
The Hothouse Archives: Plants, Pods and Panama Red, brings together artists, architects, art historians, gardeners, scientists, philosophers and cultural critics to discuss the role of plant life in contemporary culture. The conference is part of the ongoing research cooperation between the BFA Fine Arts Department at School of Visual Arts in NYC and the Institute of Art History and Modern and Contemporary Arts at University of Graz in Austria. The conference will be held in New York City.
