MA Design Research, Writing, and Criticism: New Fellowship


On Tuesday May 16, I had the pleasure of attending the MA Design Research, Writing, and Criticism thesis presentation at the SVA Theatre, “How to Nail a Hammer.”  The department’s chair, Molly Heintz, opened the event by recapping events of the year and introducing a cohort of amazing SVA graduate students who focused their research on topics intersecting with racial/ethnic discrimination, social justice, gender equity, fashion, socio-economics and illustration. Molly concluded the evening with the announcement of forthcoming postgraduate fellowships, as well as the launch of one that she has developed as a partnership with the Museum of 21st Century Design (M21D), founded by program (DCrit) alumnus William Myers (2010).  

The postgraduate museum fellowship program coaches recent MA Design Research, Writing and Criticism graduates on translating their research into proposals for exhibitions, books, convenings, podcasts and other media. The fellows are introduced to practical resources and knowledge with regard to formulating budgets, grant proposals, KPI reporting, project management and strategic communication in the context of pitching proposals. This nine-month program is tailored, to an extent, to the needs and interests of each fellow, who work with M21D curators, SVA faculty and expert guest advisors. 

MA Design Research, Writing and Criticism seeks to utilize this fellowship to support those students that identify as being from historically underrepresented groups. Heintz announced that the first recipient of the M21D Fellowship is 2022 program graduate Jared Dalcourt, whose research proposal is titled "Stereotypes: Black by Design."  

Dalcourt says his research “will venture into the terrain of American design and typographic history, uncovering its entanglements with the creation and dissemination of the deplorable 'black brute' stereotype. But this project will also be a testament to the strength and resilience of the many creators and artists who have valiantly resisted and subverted this harmful trope, casting a light on their underappreciated accomplishments and legacies.”

The M21D Fellowship will be integral with assisting Dalcourt with accomplishing his goals of developing this topic into a published book and curating an exhibition which focuses on this topic.

Special Recognition to MSU Member Brithney Rivera (BFA 2023 Computer Art, Computer Animation and Visual Effects)


Brithney Rivera, member of the Multicultural Student Union (MSU) and La Bodega student groups, served as the BFA degree candidate speaker at this year’s SVA Commencement, held at Radio City Music Hall.  The MSU is a group of concerned SVA students whose mission is to address the intersectional cultural and academic needs of BIPOC and historically underrepresented students. As a member of both MSU and La Bodega, Rivera unapologetically represented her Puerto Rican heritage through her art, student activism and voice. As a featured speaker at commencement, Rivera combined all three of these elements and executed one of the day’s most culturally engaging and uplifting speeches. At times, Rivera would accent important parts of her speech with her maraca, as well with statements in the Spanish language. We are proud of her accomplishments, and we were honored to work with her and her peers in the MSU. Congratulations!  

Pride Month June 2023


SVA Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion would like to recognize June was Pride Month. The first Pride march in New York City was held on June 28, 1970, on the one-year anniversary of the 1969 Stonewall Uprising. Notable Black and LatinX activists such as Marsha P. Johnson and Sylvia Rivera who emerged from the uprising led initiatives such as the Gay Liberation Movement and the STAR (Street Transvestite Action Revolutionaries) organization, and began the first LGBTQ+ youth shelter in North America.  

"You never completely have your rights, one person, until you all have your rights." —Marsha P. Johnson

Juneteenth with Jelani Cobb


The Dean of Columbia University's Journalism School, Jelani Cobb, provided a virtual speech about Juneteenth on June 20th. Click here to access the speech, as well as his responses to five questions that we asked him prior to his speech.

Five Questions with Jelani Cobb 


Why do you think employers should recognize Juneteenth?

Our national holidays generally serve as reminders of crucial lessons or ideas that we think of as foundational to our society. President’s Day, Memorial Day, Thanksgiving, Labor Day and Veterans Day all serve that function. Yet prior to Juneteenth there was no holiday in any way connected to the Civil War, the central conflict in American history, or the end of slavery. As a holiday, Juneteenth is probably most closely related to the 4th of July – one recognizes the founding of the nation, the other recognizes the tremendously difficult struggle that took place to make sure that nation would continue to exist and begin to live up to its founding ideals. 



How do you discuss the issues of racial injustice and the importance of Juneteenth, with those who self-identify as African-American/Black and/or non US-Citizens non-Black or non-Americans? 

I think we should be able to discuss those issues in similar ways irrespective of the audience. The end of slavery was not a triumph for African Americans. It was a triumph for a society that had belatedly embraced its founding values. One element that is particular to African Americans, however, is the shame associated with having been enslaved. It’s important to frame this as a history of survivors, not victims – people whose ingenuity and tenacity helped undermine the brutal system of bondage that might well have existed much longer than it did were it not for their determination to be free. 



With the current bans being placed on DEI Offices how is this congruent with the history legacy of social justice in states like Texas and Florida?

None of this is new. I can talk about this in more detail in person but for as long as we’ve had this history there have been people attempting to suppress the basic facts of our collective past. But doing so strips history of its primary virtue – the ability to inform people in the present of pitfalls that have preceded them. In that regard, it’s crucial that people of conscience continue making the facts of this history known and available. 



How do you envision visual arts (fine arts, screen/film, design, education) serving as a medium to highlight themes associated with Juneteenth?

I think the arts are one of our most crucial mediums for education and Juneteenth is no exception to that. There’s a virtually infinite number of themes and conversations for artists to mine as it relates to the demise of slavery and the history of Juneteenth, many of which are only just beginning to be explored. 



What is your favorite memory or tradition affiliated with Juneteenth?

I first learned of Juneteenth from a Texan classmate during my first year at Howard University. It amazed me that there was a long, independent tradition of people honoring the legacy of people who survived slavery. It was not until years later that I attended an actual celebration but my favorite memory is probably that early moment where I was introduced to it and the way that it deepened my curiosity about the threads of history so bound up with slavery, democracy, emancipation and the complicated road that we have traveled to our present moment. 

DJ Scratch: DJ Workshop and MC Competition


This August will mark the 50th anniversary of Hip-Hop. In celebration of this art form, we will be hosting several events this summer and this semester. Tomorrow, July 19 at the SVA Amphitheater (209 East 23rd Street, 3rd Floor) at 4pm we will be conducting a workshop on the art of DJing with award winning and legendary DJ and Producer, DJ Scratch. If you are and/or considered becoming a DJ, this is the perfect workshop for you. DJ Scratch will discuss aspects of the history of DJing, the art of turntablism and the scratching techniques that made him famous. Register here. 
Note: Registration ends at 4pm today.

**Freestyle MC Competition**

In addition to the DJ Workshop, there will be a Freestyle MC competition. Be sure to sign in at the sign up area upon your arrival. There will be prizes for the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd place winners. Your topic will have to be related to the 50th Anniversary of Hip Hop (the history, culture, significance, etc.) Rules will be provided the day of the event.

Bio
DJ Scratch’s illustrious career spans over three decades. From being the opening act for Run-DMC and the third member of EPMD in the ’80s, to producing eight platinum albums and earning three Grammy nominations for his work with Busta Rhymes in the ’90s, to touring as the DJ for Jay Z in the early 2000s and the opening act for Beyoncé.

Born and raised in Brooklyn, Scratch is considered in the hip-hop world as a five-star general. He is a two-time World Champion DJ, Grammy winner and the first recipient (and three-time winner) of the Global Spin Awards “Turntablist Of The Year” Award. In 2016, Scratch achieved the highest honor a DJ can receive by being knighted a Grandmaster by DJ Kool Herc, known as the father of hip-hop. While his status in hip-hop is undeniable, his talent, brand and influence extend far beyond the turntables.
