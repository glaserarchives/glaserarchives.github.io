🎙️ Get to Know: Beth Kleber, head of archives, School of Visual Arts Archives, Milton Glaser Design Study Center and Archives, Visual Arts Foundation

Tell us about your history with SVA and what your current role entails.

I’ve been kicking around SVA for a long time, first as the Picture Collection librarian in the late ’90s and other roles in the SVA Library. I left for a couple of years, but then in 2003, had the opportunity to come back and start up the Glaser Archives; how could I say no? I spent a year or so working out of Milton’s building on E. 32nd Street while he and I reviewed his vast body of work. It was a one-on-one crash course on all things Milton Glaser. Eventually, we transferred his work to the SVA Library and the collection has grown to include work from more than 20 renowned illustrators and designers. A few years later I also established the SVA Archives to document the history of the College.


“SVA❤️Milton: The Legacy of Milton Glaser” is currently up in the Gramercy Gallery and Flatiron Project Space [though temporarily closed to the public]. Can you talk about the process for putting this exhibition together from an archivist perspective?

When you celebrate Milton Glaser, you gotta play the hits, but it was important to make sure some lesser-known process works from the Archives were included, too. Kevin O’Callaghan, who designed the exhibition, had the idea of installing Milton’s actual desk, surrounded by the art-making tools and tchotchkes that inspired him. Along with the sketches, they give the exhibition a real tactile sensibility, mirroring the experience of an in-person visit to the Archives.


What are a few of your favorite pieces on display? Anything particularly unexpected?

The Tomato Records poster is an all-time favorite, it’s virtuosic and surreal and silly. I also love the very early original drawings by Milton when he was in high school and college and a recent graduate; this work, which has never been exhibited before, shows our students what Milton was doing when he was their age. We also recently digitized a batch of audio recordings of Milton’s “Design and Personality” classes at SVA; you can hear one of these recordings from 1980 play in the exhibition space and imagine you’re in the classroom with him. It’s evidence of Milton’s skill as a teacher as well as his immense personal charisma.


What do you hope visitors to the exhibition take away about Milton Glaser and his legacy with SVA and beyond?

Milton was highly engaged with the world and its cultures; he consumed literature and music, loved food, was a student of history, concerned with politics and ethics. That kind of natural curiosity and humanism seeped into everything he did and made him an exceptional teacher and a genius designer.


Anything else you want to add?

I’m excited to see how we can spread the word about SVA’s fascinating history as we head into the College’s 75th anniversary year. So many incredible people have passed through SVA’s doors.
