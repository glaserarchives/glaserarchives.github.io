Statement on Recent Police Shootings


                                                                                                                April 21, 2021
Dear Members of the SVA Community:


Just as this letter was being prepared, the world learned that a jury convicted former Minneapolis police officer Derek Chauvin of murder in the death of George Floyd.  Nearly a year after Mr. Floyd’s tragic and senseless death at the hands of police, justice has been served—in this particular case.  As we breathe a collective sigh of relief today, all the issues surrounding Mr. Floyd’s death remain largely unchanged.

In the last week, the world has witnessed two high-profile police shootings in America of unarmed youths of color.  The first instance occurred near the city of Minneapolis, just miles from where former Minneapolis police officer Derek Chauvin was on trial for the murder of George Floyd.  

Daunte Wright, 20, was shot by a 26-year veteran of the Brooklyn Center, MN, police department.  The officer claims she mistook her Glock service weapon for her taser.  Mr. Wright was killed during a traffic stop.  He was pulled over for a traffic violation related to an allegedly expired registration tag, and was being arrested for an outstanding misdemeanor warrant.

Days later in Chicago, 13-year-old Adam Toledo was shot dead by a Chicago police officer. Body camera footage appears to show that the seventh-grader complied with the officer's instructions and was apparently unarmed.  He is shown on the video with his hands raised in the air; he is not holding a gun.

According to an April 18, 2021, article in the New York Times, there have been 64 police shootings in the 20 days since the Chauvin trial began.  More than half of those killed in these shootings were Black or Latino. On average in the United States, three citizens are killed by police each day.

These police shootings have occurred against the backdrop of several mass shootings in several states—something to which we, as a society, have become all too accustomed.

The Chauvin murder trial has been a painful wound reopened, as Americans of all races and ethnicities have been forced to relive the horror of George Floyd’s death.  And these latest police shootings of unarmed Black men and boys are the salt poured into this wound, leaving so many of us to wonder if this wound will ever heal.

Last summer, massive Black Lives Matter rallies and marches protesting police violence against mostly Black and Brown people, touched off by Mr. Floyd’s murder (and the many other such killings that occurred at around the same time), took place in cities and towns across the United States—and the world. That moment was going to engender a national reckoning with America’s long history of violence against people of color, dating back to before the founding of the Republic. The Black Lives Matter (BLM) movement was elevated to new heights. We were bound and determined to use the occasion of Mr. Floyd’s death to continue a national dialogue about race.

To be fair, much has been done in the last year to confront America’s racist past, to educate ourselves about implicit bias and microaggressions, and to hold to account those individuals and institutions that continue to perpetrate racist acts or behavior. 

But the recent police killings beg the question: what will it take to end the pervasive, persistent and longstanding practice of violence against people of color by American law enforcement?  Our indignation, anger, sadness and desire for change seem not to be enough to reverse a decades-old practice of racial inequality in the world’s supposed greatest democracy.

School of Visual Arts stands resolutely against the excessive use of force by police, but especially that aimed at Black and Brown people; we support the BLM movement; and we maintain our commitment to calling out and condemning racism and bigotry in all its manifestations.  But is this enough?  What else can and should we be doing to help end institutionalized racism in the United States?  How can we as a community of artists, designers, writers and educators lead our country and the world to a place where police violence (or all violence) is relegated to the history books—and not a defining and daily reality of life for so many Americans?  As has often been repeated, this is not a black or brown issue; it is an American issue!
