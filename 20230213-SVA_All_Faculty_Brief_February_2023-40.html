All Faculty Brief

February 2023

Dear Colleagues:

I hope this installment of the All Faculty Brief finds you well.

You no doubt have seen the blizzard of articles in the popular and academic press about the quantum leap in the capabilities in Artificial Intelligence (AI). These advances have the potential to be a major disruptive force in higher education and, many argue, open up a whole new way for students to cheat, not just on written assignments but on creative projects. At the same time, AI creates opportunities for artists and designers to augment their work in exciting new ways. 

Regardless of which side of the fence you sit, SVA’s current academic integrity policy doesn’t adequately anticipate student work generated by AI platforms.  For that reason, I will be convening in late March a forum for our department chairs and others to discuss the implications of AI on our work as college educators in the liberal arts and the broad array of art and design disciplines taught at SVA.  Our goal is to develop guidance for students, faculty, and administrators on how best to navigate the largely uncharted waters of the AI space, to include ways to responsibly leverage the potential AI offers, and policy to mitigate the misuse of this technology.

The Office of Learning Technology (OLT) discusses AI and the reality and ramifications of it on students’ studio practice and liberal arts classes.

The Office of the Registrar and the Library provide important updates.

I realize the distribution of the All Faculty Brief has been sporadic this year.  Our goal is to get this newsletter out to you once a month during the academic year.


Ever grateful for all you do,

Chris

AI in Arts Education and Faculty Support From the Office of Learning Technologies

You've likely seen news articles speculating about what impact artificial intelligence (AI) will have on education or the art world. At the School of Visual Arts, the potential impact of AI is recognized in both the classroom and the professional lives of our alumni and faculty communities. While there will be an ongoing debate about the validity of AI-produced artwork, SVA faculty should be aware of the text-to-image generators available and consider how the new technology can be addressed in their classrooms. A recent Vice article shared some perspectives from art college instructors that ranged from restricting the use of text-to-image generators on class assignments to actively encouraging students to explore the new tools.

Another type of AI that recently launched, ChatGPT, can provide substantive answers to open-ended questions in a matter of seconds. ChatGPT has received lots of press ranging from the outcry that the college essay is dead (The Atlantic) to a comparison between ChatGPT and the introduction of the calculator in classrooms (MIT's CMSW). As with the faculty responses to text-to-image AI generators, responses from postsecondary institutions run the gamut from encouraging faculty to ChatGPT-proof their assignments to recognizing the potential for it to positively change teaching practices.

Given the diversity of course activities assigned at SVA, it is important to have thoughtful discussions about these new AI tools before jumping to a one-size-fits-all list of best practices. OLT encourages faculty members to attend the upcoming A.I. and the Lens & Screen Arts Symposium and to keep an eye out for faculty roundtable events that are being discussed by a few departments.

Additionally, the OLT staff is available to meet with you one-to-one or in small groups to discuss how AI tools may affect the success of your course's current assignments. Sometimes small adjustments can make a big difference in ensuring students have meaningful opportunities to get the practice needed to meet their learning goals. OLT is happy to support you in this process. Faculty members can contact OLT by:

• Scheduling a one-to-one meeting
• Visiting daily office hours in the SVA Concourse, M-F, 10-11 am and 2-3 pm
• Emailing olt@sva.edu

School of Visual Arts Archives | Milton Glaser Design Study Center and Archives

SVA has a long tradition of eccentric classes, not least of which are these two "special electives" from the 1975 registration booklet. "Practical Magic" (S-150), taught by SVA alum Leonard Giustino, was intended "to initiate the student into the age-old art of witches and warlocks," something of which the instructor, had practical knowledge. 

S-192, "Isshin-Ryu Karate" was taught at the dojo of Malachi Lee, in a building on 23rd street which has since been torn down. At 6'7", Lee was noted for his reach and height, which are on display in his sole venture into cinema, the blaxploitation martial arts film Force Four (aka Black Force), where he towers above his costars. Unfortunately, Lee died sometime in 1975, perhaps before viewing the final cut of his big-screen debut.

Updates and Reminders from the Office of the Registrar

Registration Information for the 2023-2024 Academic Year

Registration for the 2023-2024 academic year begins on March 6. You can monitor enrollment in MyServices for any classes that you are scheduled to teach. Please note that students are not allowed to register for closed classes, even with instructor approval. Students will have opportunities to add and drop courses during the official course adjustment periods.

Faculty Absence Form

As a reminder, all faculty must inform SVA when they are absent from a course they are teaching, even if a make-up session will be scheduled at a later time. To streamline this process, faculty members should complete the Faculty Absence Form when they are absent. You will need to be logged in to your MyID account to complete the form.

Attendance

Please continue to record student attendance in Canvas on a regular basis. It is extremely important for the College to receive accurate attendance in a timely manner, as we use the information to identify students who may be struggling. We have a network of administrators (advisors, student affairs staff) who provide additional support to students when warranted, which is imperative to student success efforts. Attendance data also helps us to identify students who may have stopped attending, which is incredibly important for financial aid compliance and enrollment forecasting.

Academic Unsatisfactory Progress Evaluation Form

Please note that the Academic Unsatisfactory Progress Evaluation Form is available right from your MyID dashboard. The form may be completed at any point in the semester if you feel that a student is underperforming academically. Once completed, the form is sent to the student, the student's advisor, and the Chair of the student's major department.

Please do not consider this form a penalty to your students. Rather, this is a tool to make clear to students that you have serious concerns about their academic performance and to give them actionable suggestions for what they will need to do in order to pass your class. Ideally, this notice provides students the impetus to get back on track before it is too late.
