🎙️ Get to Know: Aditi Khurana, International Regional Head, India and Singapore

Your job sounds like fun! Can you summarize what you do and a little of your history with SVA?

My job is indeed a lot of fun, as there is a lot of human interaction. India is a hub of academic excellence. My work involves branding and marketing, and reaching out to students from top international schools in India and Singapore who may be interested in pursuing their creative studies at SVA. I mentor them by guiding them through the process and giving them critical feedback on their art portfolios. I meet with parents, school counselors and sometimes even principals to help them better understand the SVA brand. It is reassuring for them to know that I will personally be present for their child in their academic future. In fact, the very first time I realized the immense trust parents put in me was when a parent said to me, "I am only sending my son to SVA because of you."


You've recruited in India, Singapore, Turkey, South Africa and China. What special skills or understanding were necessary for you to bring awareness about SVA to those countries?

My colleagues and I have one imperative thing in common for our job: To be a people person. Traveling to all these countries for work means interacting with so many different personalities belonging to unique environments and cultures. Before I travel to a new city, the critical thing for me to understand is their culture in context to visual arts. I usually try to get a sense of the students’ focus through conversations with the visual arts teacher or the school counselor, and I plan my visit accordingly to make it worthwhile for everyone.


How has the pandemic changed your approach to recruiting?

Nobody imagined schools would become virtual, but the beauty of innovation was how we adapted to these times. Acquiring knowledge and learning did not stop, so I just had to focus on identifying the proper marketing and strategy tools to help me reach my audience. I worked on my verbal communication skills, the pitch and pace of my presentation. Because of the possibility of virtual interaction, I was also able to tap India's tier two and tier three cities, so now we have students from there studying at SVA.


What do you think makes SVA an exciting prospect for an international student?

Location plays a huge role. As a city, New York is a vibrant, dynamic cultural haven that nurtures creativity and its energy is infectious. Because of its competitive power, it enables the student to develop a strong identity. With the integration of the arts with new media and commerce, it is unarguably the best place to study, practice and work as a visual artist. SVA is at the "center of the center,” giving artists an unparalleled faculty, plus the creative tools, insights and influential networks they need to develop rich aesthetic sensibilities and pursue the careers they deserve.


You recently completed a recruitment trip to the Middle East. What preparation work was required, and what are you most looking forward to experiencing?

I am really looking forward to meeting the students of the Middle East and understanding their preferences. I think there is some relatability they have to India when it comes to choosing creative arts for their undergraduate program. The essential virtues will be to hear them with patience, motivate them and give them positive feedback. The basis of all good interactions is building a trustworthy relationship and making students feel safe to open up and share their apprehensions. I hope to make an impact as a mentor and steer them to make the right choices, but I can also foresee making new friends. The last nine years with SVA have been golden, and I have made friends with so many parents, counselors and students who are now a part of my life.
