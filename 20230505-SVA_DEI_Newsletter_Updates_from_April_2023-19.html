Greetings SVA Community,

As we come to the end of another semester and school year, I cannot help but to reflect on how grateful I am to work at an institution such as SVA.  The international reach of SVA impacts so many lives and shapes culture globally.  However, it is the people within SVA that consistently create spaces of belonging and engage each other as community members.  I enjoy connecting with the security officers in the morning and when I leave, I appreciate my colleagues and the leadership who hold me accountable for fulfilling my role, and I truly appreciate the authenticity of the students who engage with all that they are in an unapologetic and genuine manner.

AAPI Month


We want to recognize that May is Asian American Pacific Islander (AAPI) Month.  APPI month recognizes the contributions made by Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders in New York, within the country, and internationally.  The American Archive of Public Broadcast provides a diverse collection of Asian American and Pacific Islander broadcasts that highlight their distinct voices and lived experiences.  

Hour of Reflection on Our Day of Healing


May is also considered Mental Health Awareness Month.  We would like to revisit a mental health event that took place last month. On April 19th, 2023, SVA's Office of DEI, Academic Affairs, Human Resources, and Student Health and Counseling Services invited the campus community (students, faculty, and staff) to meet in the Beatrice location at SVA Theatre for an Hour of Reflection on our Day of Healing. As a society, we have witnessed a series of traumatic events, such as police brutality, gun violence, natural disasters, and the systematic attack on civil rights of BIPOC, AAPI, LGBTQIA+, and our religious communities. 

In our personal and professional spaces, we have either overtly expressed our concern and/or we have dealt with these collective situations in silence.  In response to these issues, the DEI Office and SVA Community Leaders hosted a Day of Healing where we brought the campus community together to take a necessary pause to recognize the magnitude of these events. 

We invited a licensed therapist Dr. Archandria Owens and other campus leaders to create a physical and virtual space to contextualize sources of these traumas and how they are manifested within our daily lives.  Dr. Owens provided healthy strategies to assist us with learning how to heal individually, and how to heal collectively as a campus.  Special appreciation to our Provost Christopher Cyphers, Executive Director of Human Resources Vennette Jones, and Associate Director of Student Health and Counseling Services by being present, sharing encouraging words, and providing resources for those in need of support.
If you missed this phenomenal session, please feel free to access it via your Kaltura Media Space by clicking Hour of Reflection.  Please note that only SVA community members (students, faculty, and staff) can access the video.  We do not yet have the rights to share the session publicly.

Earth Day Teach In, Humanities & Sciences and DEI


Earth Day began in 1970 as a response to the environmental conditions that were affecting the planet.  Unregulated uses of various energy sources polluted the air, water, and soil.  This pollution adversely affected the health and lives of many Americans.  The first Earth Day, inspired by Senator Gaylord Nelson, junior senator from Wisconsin, took place on over 1500 college campuses.  He and other legislators recruited young activists and college students to organize a teach-in to focus on the current conditions and find solutions to these issues.

On April 24, the Humanities and Science Department, SVA Library, and the Office of Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion sponsored their own teach-in, which was organized by SVA faculty member, Quito Ziegler.  Quito invited international environmentalists from South America, Gloria Ushigua, Rossana Huenufil, and Dani Valdovinos to speak on the environmental conditions in their respective communities.  Similar to the first Earth Day, these activists shared how their environment, culture, and habitat were being affected by the depletion of natural resources and how they were in battle with international corporations.  

In addition to environmental justice based discussions, Quito also provided space for SVA students to speak from and about their lived experiences and how their intersectional identities are marginalized by laws, policies, and practices that are contrary to their beliefs and liberties.  These students demonstrated poise and courage while sharing narratives associated with invisibility, violence, and isolation.
